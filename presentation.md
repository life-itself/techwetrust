# In Tech We Trust — Presentation

## Talk overview

~20-25 minutes. Core takeaway: you cannot govern technology without shifting the cultural paradigm.

---

## 1. The Wisdom Gap (3-4 min)

**Open hard. Set the stakes.**

Our powers keep outrunning our capacity to use them wisely — and the gap is accelerating.

- **Nuclear weapons**: physicists split the atom "without any hesitations the very moment they knew how to do it" (Hannah Arendt) — despite knowing the destructive potential. We've spent 80 years trying to manage a technology we unleashed in a heartbeat.
- **Climate**: at the highest levels of climate policy, our plans increasingly rely on *future technological breakthroughs* in carbon removal that don't exist yet. We're betting civilisation on faith in tech to fix what tech broke.
- **Smartphones & youth**: we handed addictive technology to entire populations of children. No experts in the effects on youth mental health existed to consult — because the technology arrived before the expertise.
- **AI**: "Humanity is about to be handed almost unimaginable power, and it is deeply unclear whether our social, political, and technological systems possess the maturity to wield it." (Dario Amodei, head of Anthropic)

Each time the pattern repeats: unleash first, understand later. And each time, the stakes get higher.

---

## 2. Why Does This Keep Happening?

### 2a. The Runaway Horse — technology from servant to master

> *A person is sitting on a horse, galloping very quickly. At a crossroads, a friend shouts, "Where are you going?" The man says, "I don't know, ask the horse!"*
> — Thich Nhat Hanh

Technology was once a powerful servant — heating our homes, curing disease, connecting us. But somewhere along the way, it crossed a line. We think we're still in the saddle, but the horse is choosing the direction.

We tell ourselves a comforting story: a need arises, innovation meets it, and rational humans choose what's best. But look around. We've normalised addiction to our smartphones. We "consume" our world through algorithmic feeds that hijack our attention and sell it for profit. We don't question it — because the story says we're choosing freely.

**The sorcerer's apprentice dynamic**: We unleash a technology. It creates problems. We unleash more technology to fix those problems. The problems compound. Climate is the clearest case — tech-fuelled destruction of the biosphere, and the "solution" at COP is to bet on future tech that doesn't exist yet. Social media → algorithmic disinformation → AI fact-checkers → deeper distortion of the information landscape. Each fix creates the next crisis.

**Story: The Zen Master visits Google (2013)**

Thich Nhat Hanh was invited to Google HQ. The conversation turned to the harm of addictive apps. He suggested: *mobile phones could easily have a mindfulness bell — a sound every quarter hour to interrupt the addictive pattern and recall people to themselves.*

The engineer objected: *"But wouldn't we be imposing something people don't need?"*

The Zen Master replied: *"It's not so simple. People's responses can come from real needs or false needs. Hunger can signal a real need to eat. But many people eat to escape their suffering. The same is true with technology. You're often serving sensations to people to conceal their suffering — loneliness, anxiety. You have to help people distinguish."*

The engineer's objection is the modern paradigm in miniature: *we don't impose, we let people choose freely.* But choice is useless without the capacity to choose well. We believe ourselves fully rational and fully agentic — and this belief blinds us to the ways technology distorts our agency.

### 2b. It's worse than that: Tech as God

You might think the problem is just that technology is out of control — a runaway horse we need to rein in. But it goes deeper. We don't just *fail* to control technology — we actively *worship* it.

Modern humanity relates to technology as a surrogate god:

- **Mystery and awe**: "There's no discernible difference between the internet and an omniscient being — except that the former answers questions with a higher degree of reliability. I don't ask how it works. I just switch it on and trust."
- **Salvation**: companies offering cryopreservation at death, transhumanists imagining silicon consciousness — ideas that evolved "from crackpot to merely eccentric." The transcendental hopes that once energised mass worship now have a technological basis.
- **High priests**: the most famous, most powerful people in our world are tech founders. From Steve Jobs to Elon Musk — alive or dead, their opinion is thought relevant in every matter. We look to technologists for answers the way previous ages looked to clergy. It's no coincidence Musk now runs a "Department of Government Efficiency," reshaping the state itself.
- **Blind faith in crisis**: even facing existential climate breakdown, our best policy plans assume *more* tech will save us from tech. We try to solve the problem with more of the problem — and we do this because we *believe*.

The horse isn't just running away from us. We're *worshipping* the horse.

### What's underneath both metaphors

These aren't just bad habits. They're expressions of deep features of the modern cultural paradigm:

- **Materialism**: reality reduced to what can be measured
- **The myth of progress**: more tech is always better, questioning it is naive
- **Rationalism**: we're rational agents making free choices (so no need for guardrails)
- **Individualism**: separate selves competing for advantage
- **Homo economicus**: the sovereign consumer knows what's good for them; any harm is an "externality"

Technology didn't just arise *within* this paradigm — it has become its chief organising logic, reciprocally shaping how we see reality.

## 3. The Trap: Why Governance Alone Can't Work (5-6 min)

So why can't we just... regulate better? Govern more wisely?

### The race to the bottom

This is a collective action problem. In the AI conversation, even among senior industry figures, the question keeps arising: *given the risk, should we pause?*

The answer is always the same: *"We can't. If we pause, someone else won't."*

If OpenAI doesn't, Google will. If Google doesn't, the next venture-funded startup will. No major actor is incentivised to slow down. Government *could* intervene — but legislation is beholden to short-term profit, and manufacturers increasingly litigious.

> *"If you had a peaceful civilisation and Genghis Khan wanted that area... your peaceful civilisation was wiped out. The same is true if you want to internalise the cost of carbon: your country gets destroyed by whoever externalises that cost. The thing that has been more successfully dominant, extracted more, grown its violence capability, wins. And that thing plus exponential tech, at planetary boundaries, self-terminates."*
> — Daniel Schmachtenberger

**Story: The fort**

Imagine defending a fort together. If everyone stays, some may die but most survive and the enemy is repelled. Anyone who leaves may save themselves — provided others stay behind. But if enough people try to save themselves, the battle is lost and *everyone* dies.

This is exactly where we are with AI, climate, and nuclear weapons — at planetary scale.

### The root: a worldview of separateness

These collective action problems aren't just coordination failures you can engineer your way out of. They're rooted in a worldview that sees reality as fundamentally divided: separate individuals, competing for advantage.

Every attempt to fix tech governance *within* this paradigm reproduces the paradigm. Alignment research. Effective altruism. Rational game theory. They all operate the engineering mindset and then try to fix problems created by that mindset.

**Story: The Balinese water temples**

For over a thousand years, the Subak irrigation system in Bali has solved what should be an intractable collective action problem. Individual farmers must limit their water use and maintain their segment of the system. All would benefit short-term from free-riding. Yet the system works.

Why? It's not just irrigation — it's a network of *water temples*. The whole system expresses a philosophy (*Tri Hita Karana*) uniting spirit, the human world, and nature. Rice is a gift from god. Water temple rituals emphasise dependence on the living world.

The cooperation didn't come from rational incentive design. It came from shared meaning, shared identity, shared sense of belonging to something larger. *That's* what overcomes collective action problems — not better game theory.

## 4. The Takeaway (2-3 min)

**If you take away one thing from this talk:**

You cannot govern technology wisely without shifting the cultural paradigm that produces our dysfunctional relationship with it.

The attempts to regulate, align, and govern technology — however well-intentioned — will keep failing as long as they operate within the worldview of separate, competing, rational individuals. You can't fix a paradigm problem with paradigm tools.

A future society that survives its own technologies will be one that has undergone a deeper shift — in how we understand ourselves, each other, and our relationship to the living world.

## 5. A Wiser Path — Sketched (3-4 min)

*[Flag: this deserves its own full session / paper. Here we sketch the direction.]*

What would a wiser relationship with technology look like?

### Slowness and spaciousness
It's difficult to be wise in a rush. A wiser society would prioritise reflection over instant gratification — pausing to ask "is this what we want?" before unleashing new powers. Imagine a moratorium: six months for philosophers, spiritual leaders, technologists, facilitators to ask together: *do we want this? Do we want it now? How do we contain it?*

### A fuller account of human nature
Beyond homo economicus — restoring value to intuition, embodied knowing, emotional intelligence, and the subtle inner senses that help us discern what's genuinely good. These aren't soft extras. They're essential capacities for wise choice-making that our rationalist culture has systematically sidelined.

### Interbeing
The Buddhist concept coined by Thich Nhat Hanh: nothing exists in isolation. "To be is always to inter-be." This isn't just a lovely idea — it's a truth about reality. And it contains a simple, devastating insight: *nobody wins a competition that ends in shared destruction.*

A worldview alive to interbeing makes collective action problems dissoluble — because when you perceive identity *with* each other and the world, there are no externalities.

### Closing image

> The godlike status of technology has become a vicious circle. The more machines empower us, the more willingly we defer to them — rendering ourselves less capable of resisting technological momentum. Restoring wise relationship with technology first requires transforming our impoverished, computational view of what it means to be human.

A future society that survives will restore technology as servant to what is most human, most relational, and most conducive to life.
