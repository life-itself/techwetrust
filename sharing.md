## Appendix: The runaway horse — why "we can't stop it" is the problem

There are two versions of why we can't govern technology wisely.

The first is dramatic: we worship technology like a god. We trust it, revere it, defer to it. That's the "In Tech We Trust" thesis in its strongest form — and it's true, but not everyone sees themselves in it.

The second is gentler, and maybe more insidious: we've decided technology is simply unstoppable. Not a god we worship, but a force we can't stand in the way of. A runaway horse. Even people who don't think they worship tech often feel this — that it's bigger than us, that it's just going to keep happening, that resistance is futile.

You hear it constantly in the AI conversation: "There's no point regulating AI because we can't." But notice what's happening in that sentence. It's a classic confusion between what is desirable and what we think is possible. We allow our sense of what's possible to quietly undermine our thinking about what's desirable. "We shouldn't bother" slides into "we can't" — and once you've arrived at "we can't," you stop trying.

But is it actually true? If 80% of the world's population tomorrow wanted us to stop building faster AI and faster chips, we could stop. That's obviously true. The obstacle is not capability. The obstacle is that we're embedded in a culture that has made technological acceleration feel like a law of nature — inevitable, irresistible, like gravity.

It isn't. It's a choice we keep making, shaped by a paradigm we rarely examine. The horse isn't actually in charge. We just forgot we're holding the reins.
